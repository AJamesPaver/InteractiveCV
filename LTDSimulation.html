Longitudinal Train Dynamics Simulation

This simulation demonstrates a physics based model of a battery-electric heavy haul train, consisting of two locomotives - one leading and one trailing - and eighteen ore cars.


The simulation predicts the dynamic in-train forces and energy usage as the consist hauls unloaded wagons uphill, and returns loaded wagons downhill.


Such simulations show how physics modelling, together with data visualisation, allow for the prediction of complex constraints in electro-mechanical systems which must be considered when optimising the design and operation of dynamic systems.

Example Longitudinal Electric Haul Train Simulation with Regen capability.

The design of a new battery-electric heavy haul locomotive is an incredibly complex, multifaceted project with many different, non-intuitive constraints.


The purpose of data science and physics simulation, is to provide the team with the most valuable insight to be able to best position the product for the chosen application. Taking into account electrical, mechanical, thermal, financial, operational and other constraints.

One of the key constraints of heavy-haul locomotives is the in-train forces. Heavy-haul trains are normally hundreds of cars long, weighing tens-of-thousands of tons, spanning multiple kilometers in a single moving vehicle!


The resulting forces are non-linear, complex to predict and depend on:

  • The consist configuration (number and locations of ore cars and locomotives in the train)
  • The masses of each of the cars.
  • The tractive effort and control behaviour of the locomotives - synchronous vs. asynchronous control.
  • The track topology in grade and curvature.
  • The resistance forces (rolling and aerodynamic) of each of the vehicles in the train.
  • The non-linear dynamics of the drawbar mechanisms.

These forces must be kept within limits at all times during the operation of the train. This can have a severe impact on the amount of energy which can be regenerated whilst driving loaded downhill.

This simulation (written in Python and C++) represents such a multi-vehicle train system where each locomotive and ore car follows a defined distance-vs-elevation profile.


The locomotives attempt to follow a speed-profile, generating motor torque with an electric motor model and regenerating electrical power when braking downhill, returning the energy to the battery system.

The simulation computes each of the drawbar forces along the train, both compressive and tensile, and predicts the dynamics of the ore cars which are pushed and pulled by these drawbars.


A linked 3D animation built with Three.js visualises the train travelling along its route. A series of Plotly.js charts display the numerical data from the simulation and update the key metrics in real-time alongside the 3D animation, including battery state-of-charge, vehicle speed and drawbar forces along the entire train.
