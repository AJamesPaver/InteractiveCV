1D Electric Vehicle Simulation

Take a look at this example I've written below of a truck simulation driving up and down a slope.


This simple model considers the variable mass of the vehicle (with payload), the terrain slope angle, the rolling resistance forces from the tyres, the behaviour of a simple electric motor, the electrical efficiency (in both propulsion and regen) and ancillary power draw from the battery.

Example 1D Electric Truck Simulation with Regen capability.

Electric Heavy Industry vehicles are incredibly complex and costly to develop. Simulation empowers the engineering team to define requirements, perform attribute trade-offs and evalute the Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) of the system.


At the heart of the simulation is the vehicle-dynamics model, this is what drives the vehicle over the defined terrain. In the case of mining haul-trucks, the performance of the vehicle driving up and down slopes can be very influential. The Liebherr T264 haul truck can take up to 240 tonnes of payload!


The vehicle dynamics model considers the forces on the body of the vehicle, the torque provided by the motors and how that transfers to the wheels and the wheel & tyre forces including rolling resistance.


The electrical model then considers the power required to drive the motors and the all-important efficiencies of each component in the powertrain. That power is then provided by the battery as either a discharge power, or a regenerative recharge power.


Notice how the battery power behaves differently when the truck is loaded with payload and going downhill. The truck is able to regen and recharge some of the battery.

In order to ensure that we are making the correct decisions, we must take into account the entire system. It's no good optimising one part of the system, if the impact of that will be detrimental to another system. In engineering, we call this a "full" optimisation, as opposed to a "partial" optimisation.


The performance simulation team, therefore, must simulate the full electric vehicle including its powertrain, energy usage, thermals, masses, inertias and forces. Only then can we be sure we are making the correct performance trade-offs for the project.

The creation of such a complex vehicle model requires very good collaboration across the departments at Fortescue. This is one of the most rewarding parts of the job and something I focus a lot of my effort on.


It takes a lot of very talented people to create the insights which will yield the best results. The key to success in Performance Simulation is creating good relationships with the other teams so that we can all contribute to the overall "big picture" for product design.
